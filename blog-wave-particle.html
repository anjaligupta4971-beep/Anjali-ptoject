Wave–Particle Duality and Electron Diffraction

Topic: Quantum / Waves · Level: Intro to Modern Physics

In classical physics, waves and particles are treated as very different objects. A particle such as a small steel ball travels along a trajectory, while a water wave spreads out and shows interference when it meets an obstacle. In quantum physics, experiments show that microscopic particles such as electrons can display both types of behaviour.

Electron Diffraction

When a beam of electrons is directed at a thin crystal, such as a graphite foil, a pattern of bright and dark rings can be observed on a fluorescent screen. This pattern is very similar to the diffraction pattern obtained when light waves pass through a grating, and it is strong evidence that electrons have wave-like properties.

The de Broglie relation assigns a wavelength \(\lambda\) to a particle of momentum \(p\): \[ \lambda = \dfrac{h}{p}, \] where \(h\) is Planck's constant. For electrons accelerated through a potential difference, this wavelength is small but still large enough to be detected in diffraction experiments.

Interpreting the Wave

The wave associated with an electron is described mathematically by a wavefunction \(\psi\). The probability of detecting the electron at a particular position is proportional to \(|\psi|^2\). In a diffraction experiment, the bright regions of the pattern correspond to positions where \(|\psi|^2\) is large due to constructive interference.

Single-Electron Build Up

A striking demonstration of wave–particle duality occurs when the experiment is carried out using a very low intensity electron beam. At first, single dots appear one by one on the detector, consistent with particle-like impacts. Over time, however, the dots build up to form the same interference pattern observed with a more intense beam.

This behaviour suggests that each electron passes through the crystal as a delocalized wave, but is detected as a localized particle. The interference pattern reflects the wave nature, while the individual detection events show the particle nature.
