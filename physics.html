Physics Lab: Mechanics & Circuits

This section collects key formulas, short explanations, and lab-style calculators for introductory mechanics and basic electric circuits.

1. Kinematics: Constant Acceleration

Kinematics describes motion in terms of displacement \(s\), velocity \(v\), and acceleration \(a\) without explaining why the motion occurs. For motion in a straight line with constant acceleration, the following equations are commonly used:

  • \( v = u + at \)
  • \( s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \)
  • \( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)

Here, \(u\) is the initial velocity, \(v\) is the final velocity, \(t\) is the time interval, and \(s\) is the displacement.

Vertical Drop Time Calculator

Assuming an object is dropped from rest \(u = 0\) and falls freely under gravity \(g = 9.81\,\text{m s}^{-2}\), the time to fall from height \(h\) is \[ t = \sqrt{\dfrac{2h}{g}}. \]

2. Energy: Kinetic and Potential

The kinetic energy of a particle of mass \(m\) moving with speed \(v\) is \[ E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2. \] Near Earth's surface, the gravitational potential energy of an object of mass \(m\) at height \(h\) is \[ E_p = mgh. \]

3. Circuits: Ohm's Law

Ohm's law connects voltage \(V\), current \(I\), and resistance \(R\) in many simple circuits: \[ V = IR. \]

If any two of \(V\), \(I\), and \(R\) are known, the third can be calculated. For example, \( I = V/R \) and \( R = V/I \).

4. Waves: Wavelength and Frequency

Waves are periodic disturbances that transfer energy without transferring matter. The relationship between wavelength \(\lambda\), frequency \(f\), and wave speed \(c\) is: \[ c = \lambda f. \] For electromagnetic waves in vacuum, \(c = 3.00 \times 10^8\) m/s.

The amplitude of a wave is the maximum displacement from equilibrium. For sound waves, amplitude relates to loudness; for light waves, it relates to brightness.

5. Thermodynamics: Heat and Energy

The first law of thermodynamics states that the change in internal energy \(\Delta U\) of a system equals the heat added \(Q\) minus the work done by the system \(W\): \[ \Delta U = Q - W. \]

Heat capacity \(C\) relates the heat added to a system to its temperature change: \[ Q = C\Delta T. \] For a specific substance, the specific heat capacity \(c\) is used: \[ Q = mc\Delta T, \] where \(m\) is mass and \(c\) is the specific heat capacity.
