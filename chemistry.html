1. Atomic Structure

Atoms consist of a small, positively charged nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons occupying quantized energy levels. The atomic number \(Z\) equals the number of protons and defines the element, while the mass number \(A\) is the total number of protons and neutrons.

In many college chemistry problems, electron configurations are written using subshell notation such as \(1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^2\). The valence electrons (outermost shell) largely determine chemical reactivity.

3. Solution Chemistry

Molarity \(M\) is defined as the amount of solute in moles per liter of solution: \[ M = \dfrac{n}{V}. \] Here, \(n\) is the number of moles and \(V\) is the solution volume in liters.

5. Acid-Base Chemistry

Acids are substances that donate protons (H⁺ ions), while bases accept protons. The pH scale measures acidity: \[ \text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}^+], \] where \([\text{H}^+]\) is the hydrogen ion concentration in mol/L.

Strong acids (e.g., HCl, H₂SO₄) completely dissociate in water, while weak acids (e.g., CH₃COOH) only partially dissociate. The acid dissociation constant \(K_a\) quantifies this: \[ K_a = \frac{[\text{H}^+][\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}. \]

6. Redox Reactions

Oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions involve the transfer of electrons between species. Oxidation is the loss of electrons, while reduction is the gain of electrons. The species that loses electrons is the reducing agent, and the species that gains electrons is the oxidizing agent.

In a balanced redox reaction, the number of electrons lost must equal the number of electrons gained. For example, in the reaction: \[ \text{Zn} + \text{Cu}^{2+} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + \text{Cu}, \] zinc is oxidized (loses 2 electrons) and copper is reduced (gains 2 electrons).

Oxidation numbers help track electron transfer. Rules include: elements in their standard state have an oxidation number of 0, oxygen is usually -2, and hydrogen is usually +1 (except in metal hydrides where it is -1).
