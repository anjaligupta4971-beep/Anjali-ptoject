Interpreting the Ideal Gas Law at Particle Level

Topic: Physical Chemistry / Kinetic Theory · Level: General Chemistry

The ideal gas law, \[ pV = nRT, \] relates the pressure \(p\), volume \(V\), amount of gas \(n\), temperature \(T\), and the gas constant \(R\). It summarizes how a gas sample responds when these macroscopic quantities are changed.

Microscopic Picture

In the kinetic theory of gases, a gas consists of many small particles (atoms or molecules) moving randomly. The pressure exerted on the walls of a container arises from collisions of these particles with the walls.

For an ideal gas, particles are assumed to have negligible volume compared to the container and to experience no intermolecular forces except during collisions. Under these assumptions, it can be shown that the average translational kinetic energy is \[ \langle E_k \rangle = \tfrac{3}{2}k_B T, \] where \(k_B\) is Boltzmann's constant.

Connecting to Temperature

Temperature in this model is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles. Raising the temperature increases the typical speed of the molecules, which leads to more frequent and more energetic collisions with the container walls and therefore higher pressure if volume is held constant.

When the Ideal Gas Law Breaks Down

Real gases deviate from ideal behaviour at high pressures and low temperatures, where the assumptions of negligible particle volume and zero intermolecular forces are no longer valid. Under those conditions more detailed equations of state, such as the van der Waals equation, are used.
